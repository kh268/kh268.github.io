Research Philosophy

Volume IV | Philosophy of Science

The Philosophy of Curiosity-Driven Science.

Author: Quang Kien Han | Minerva University | Computational Science

To study the universe is to engage in a dialogue with silence. In my work, curiosity is not merely a spark of interest but a rigorous methodology. We often view mathematics as a tool for calculation, but in its purest form, it is the language of universal constraints — it defines the boundaries of what is possible within the fabric of our existence.

Equation I: Optimization Primitive
\(\min_{x \in \mathcal{F}} \sum_{e \in E} c_e x_e\)

My philosophy posits that research should seek the 'What' and 'Why' of underlying mechanisms — not only the immediate 'How'. Understanding mathematical constraints grants foresight to build systems harmonious with the world.

The 'Why' behind my work centers on the belief that complexity is an emergent property of simple, immutable laws. When we look at an integral, we are not just summing infinitesimal parts; we are observing the accumulation of reality across a continuous dimension.

Integral Calculus
\(\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx\)

The accumulation of infinitesimal truths — a metaphor for how small, rigorous observations build a complete picture of reality.

Asymptotic Complexity
\(T(n) = O(n \log n)\)

Efficiency is not a luxury but a structural necessity. Understanding growth rates shapes what problems we can even aspire to solve.

Glossary Item 01

Curiosity-Driven: Research initiated by the investigator's own interest rather than industrial or clinical application requirements.

Annotation

Constraints are not barriers, but the very scaffolding of reality.

Graph Algorithms Benchmarking

Topological Network Complexity Map

Studied bipartite matching and minimum-cost flow through the lens of classical TCS. Implemented and benchmarked algorithms across the computational hierarchy — from the foundational Bellman-Ford relaxation framework (shortest augmenting paths, \(O(VE)\)) to Gabow's scaling algorithm for weighted matching, and Goldberg-Tarjan cost-scaling for min-cost flow (\(O(nm \log n \log(nC))\)). Investigated convergence behavior as a function of graph structure and edge-weight distribution.

Biographical Monograph

Quang Kien Han

Subjectivity, Systemic Hardship, and the Return to Inquiry.

01. Origin

My earliest memories are textured by the rough bark of rubber trees and the biting scent of lime powder.

I would ask my older neighbors for their old math magazines, reading them with sheer eagerness. One day, I stumbled upon a short article about applying lime water and copper(II) sulfate to rubber trees. It reminded me of how my grandmother used to sprinkle lime powder around the base of her banana trees whenever their leaves turned yellow. So I decided to experiment — with the naive curiosity of a child, just to see if it could somehow help the farmers in my rural hometown.

02. Migration

When I entered high school, I lived away from home for the first time and had to face the complexities of life. Learning gradually became merely a tool to achieve practical ends. I only studied when I knew exactly what the payoff would be, rarely allowing myself to dig deeper into anything that wouldn't appear on an exam.

Then came college in San Francisco. The time I spent living in the Tenderloin neighborhood gave me invaluable experiences. I lived alongside some of the most marginalized people in the world — people under the crushing weight of systemic forces with no clear way to break free, yet who still held onto a genuine love for life, cherishing the smallest joys: a day without rain, a full meal, a blanket with fewer tears in it.

"The city is a laboratory where the variables are humans, and the catalysts are often cruel."

It was then that I understood, more clearly than ever, the immense privileges I held. I gained a profound appreciation for every book I read and every experience I lived through.

03. Synthesis

Casually dropping by a thrift store on Mission Street one day, I found myself drawn to a stack of frayed, worn-out science books. I brought a thick pile home and, once again, immersed myself in the history of Pi, the Solar System, spacetime, and the absolute limits of light.

I decided to major in Mathematics, not for any guarantee of a secure future, but out of a deep desire to understand the world more profoundly every single day. I was no longer in a rush to figure out what learning would bring me; instead, I started learning for the sake of the unsolved questions themselves.

And perhaps, after a long journey, that is how I finally found my way back to myself.
